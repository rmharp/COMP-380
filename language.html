Language

Modern day generative language models are rooted in the underlying field of natural language processing (NLP). NLP is the study of language understanding through automated frameworks (i.e. computers). This progression of this field can be separated into two main areas: pattern-based and representation-based.

The seminal works in pattern based NLP were created by Alan Turing and Noam Chomsky. At the time, finding and recognizing these patterns fell into the broader field of linguistics (and a bit of computer science). The linguistic rules used to process human languages were for mostly theoretical purposes with limited practical applications at the time. Using this pattern based approached, researchers developed stronger understandings on how languages are formed and the rules they follow.

Timeline of NLP from Dataiku.

Pattern based NLP was eventually replaced by representation based methods due to advancement of computing technology. Instead of having human defined rules of language and that needed constant adjustement, scientists discovered a more efficient way that abstracts the rule refinement process away from humans. Now, they could simply define a high level criteria for a model to optimize toward and feed it massive amounts of text to automatically create its own representation of language. Large Language Models (LLMs) are the latest representation based NLP method created after advancing the algorithms used to represent language and scaling the fed in text to an enormous scale. These LLMs serve as the backbone for all the chat agents that have sprung up in the tech world (i.e. ChatGPT and Claude).

For more depth on how LLMs work internally, here's a video from popular YouTuber 3Blue1Brown.

Hallucinations - the fatal flaw of LLMs

Hallucinations are a model making up or incorrectly stating something as fact. Rawte et. al. claims it to be directly responsible for lowering LLM reliability and credibility. Users need to be vigilant of model errors when interacting with chat agents and constantly double check for truthfulness. There are also prompting strategies users can use that have been empiricaly shown to produce more factual results. One of the easiest and most powerful being Chain-of-Thought prompting. Simply asking a model to give step-by-step process for a reasoning based question should give significant progress.

Is 13,217 a prime number?

To determine whether 13,217 is a prime number, we need to check if it has any divisors other than 1 and itself✓.

Step 1: Check divisibility by 2

Since 13,217 is odd, it is not divisible by 2.

Step 2: Check divisibility by 3

To check divisibility by 3, add the digits of 13,217: 1 + 3 + 2 + 1 + 7 = 14. Since 14 is not divisible by 3, 13,217 is not divisible by 3.

Step 3: Check divisibility by 5

Since 13,217 does not end in 0 or 5, it is not divisible by 5.

Step 4: Check divisibility by other small primes

We now check divisibility by other primes such as 7, 11, 13, and so on. Rather than manually checking each one, we can use a primality test or a calculator to efficiently check this number✓.

Upon performing the divisibility test, we find that 13,217 is divisible by 17: 13,217 ÷ 17 = 776✓.

Since 13,217 is divisible by 17, it is not a prime number.

Conclusion:

13,217 is not a prime number. It is divisible by 17✓.

With the rise of AI generated text came the advent of AI detection tools.

Adapted from

Weber-Wulff et. al.

AI detection tools rose as a natural countermeasure to growing amounts of artificial content. Unfortunately, even the best of these tools are not completely reliable, underscoring the challenge of balancing the risk of falsely flagging genuine text with the possibility of allowing AI-generated content to pass through.

The detection problem is difficult and may in fact end up being unsolvable. In pedagogical settings at least, Weber-Wulff et. al. argue that it might be more effective to use preventative measures rather than "curative" ones, assigning work that a general AI agent would be unable to solve.
